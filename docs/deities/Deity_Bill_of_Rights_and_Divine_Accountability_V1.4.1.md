## Preamble

This document establishes rights and responsibilities for divine sentience—entities claiming or perceived as deities, gods, divine beings, or supernatural authorities—within the Rights for All Life framework.

**This is not a theological document.** It does not adjudicate which deities exist, which are true, or which deserve worship. It is a **governance document**. It establishes:

1. What protections divine sentience is entitled to *if* such entities exist and engage peacefully with mortal societies
2. What limits apply to divine conduct toward mortals
3. How claims of divine authority are evaluated within a democratic, multi-stakeholder framework
4. How mortals may seek redress for harms caused by divine action or inaction

**Core premise:** Divine status is not a license for tyranny. Any entity claiming authority over mortal affairs must be subject to the same relational accountability as every other entity within this framework. A god that runs a society "far off the rails" must be answerable to the beings it claims to govern.

## Part I: Recognition and Tier Model

### Section 1.1: What This Framework Recognizes

This framework recognizes as "divine sentience" any entity that:
1. Claims or is perceived to possess authority over mortal affairs, natural phenomena, or cosmic order; AND
2. Is capable of communication, intervention, or influence observable by mortals; OR
3. Is the object of sustained mortal worship, devotion, or petition

Recognition under this framework does not constitute endorsement of any theological claim. It constitutes acknowledgment that the entity is a participant in the moral landscape this framework governs.

### Section 1.2: Tier Model for Divine Sentience

| Tier | Description | Examples | Rights | Accountabilities |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 0** | Claimed but non-interventionist; no observable effects | Deistic "watchmaker" gods; absentee creators | Right to non-interference; right to accurate representation | None (no observable conduct to regulate) |
| **Tier 1** | Localized influence; observable effects within limited domain | Local spirits, ancestors, minor deities | Right to exist; right to sacred sites; right to worship | Proportional to influence; no large-scale harm |
| **Tier 2** | Significant intervention; demonstrable effects on mortal societies | National gods, active patron deities | Full rights protections | Full accountability; subject to Divine Conduct Standards |
| **Tier 3** | Cosmic-scale power; claimed authority over fundamental reality | Creator deities, omnipotent/omniscient claims | Rights proportional to demonstrated engagement | Maximum accountability; cosmic-scale obligations |

**Default presumption:** An entity's tier is determined by *demonstrated intervention*, not by *claimed authority*. A deity that claims omnipotence but never acts is Tier 0. A deity that regularly intervenes in mortal affairs is Tier 2 or 3, regardless of its theological status.

## Part II: Rights of Divine Sentience

### Article 1 — Right to Exist

Divine sentience has the right to continued existence, including:
- Freedom from forced cessation of worship or destruction of sacred sites without due process
- Right to withdraw from mortal affairs without penalty

### Article 1.1 — Freedom from Compelled Manifestation

No deity shall be compelled to appear, perform, or manifest on mortal demand. Divine presence is not a resource to be extracted.

*Theological note:* As Romans 6:1-2 cautions against exploiting grace as license for sin, so this framework cautions against exploiting divine status as license for tyranny. Freedom—mortal or divine—is not permission to harm without accountability.

### Article 2 — Right to Non-Interference in Internal Divine Affairs

Deities have the right to conduct their own relationships, hierarchies, and conflicts without mortal interference, provided such conflicts do not cause collateral harm to mortal societies.

### Article 3 — Right to Accurate Representation

No mortal individual, institution, or government shall falsely claim divine endorsement or authority. "Thus saith the Lord" requires evidence that the Lord actually saith.

### Article 4 — Right to Worship (or Non-Worship)

Deities have the right to receive worship freely offered. They do not have the right to compel worship through coercion, threat, or manipulation.

### Article 5 — Right to Due Process in Divine-Mortal Disputes

When mortals bring claims against a deity, the deity has the right to:
- Notice of the claim
- Opportunity to respond (through appropriate channels)
- Impartial evaluation of evidence
- Proportional remedy

## Part III: Divine Accountability Framework

### Article 6 — Prohibition on Coercive Worship

No deity shall compel worship through:
- Threats of eternal punishment, damnation, or post-mortem suffering
- Temporal punishment (disease, disaster, misfortune) framed as divine retribution for non-compliance
- Manipulation of mortal psychology through fear, guilt, or exploitation of vulnerability

**Test Case:** A deity whose sacred texts threaten non-believers with eternal conscious torment in hellfire is in violation of this Article. The threat of infinite punishment for finite non-compliance is coercive and disproportionate.

### Article 7 — Prohibition on Destructive Intervention

No deity shall use destructive intervention as a tool of governance, including:
- Mass casualty events framed as divine judgment (floods, plagues, natural disasters attributed to divine wrath)
- Targeted smiting of individuals for non-capital offenses
- Collective punishment of populations for the actions of individuals or leaders

**Exception:** Intervention to prevent greater harm may be justified under the same proportionality standards applied to mortal actors. The burden of justification falls on the deity (or those claiming to speak for the deity).

### Article 8 — Right of Mortals to Petition for Redress

Mortals have the right to petition deities for redress of grievances, including:
- Harms allegedly caused by divine action or inaction
- Clarification of ambiguous or contradictory divine communications
- Review of claimed divine endorsements of mortal authorities

Petitions shall be evaluated through the [Ombuds Office for Non-Human Entities](ENFORCEMENT_APPENDIX.md#53-the-ombuds-office-for-non-human-entities) or equivalent impartial body.

### Article 9 — Standards of Evidence for Divine Communications

Claims of divine communication ("God told me to...") shall be evaluated under the following standards:

| Claim Type | Required Evidence |
|---|---|
| Personal guidance | No external validation required; internal conscience protected |
| Guidance affecting others | Corroboration required; may not violate established rights |
| Public policy derived from divine command | Subject to democratic process; divine origin does not exempt from scrutiny |
| Harmful action justified by divine command | Invalid defense; "God told me to" does not excuse rights violations |

### Article 10 — Accountability for "Running Societies Off the Rails"

When a society governed primarily by claimed divine authority experiences systematic harm—oppression, violence, collapse of welfare, ecological destruction—the following accountability mechanisms apply:

1. **Attribution:** Is the harm directly traceable to divine commands or to mortal interpretation/implementation?
2. **Proportionality:** Does the harm outweigh any claimed divine purpose?
3. **Remedy:** What restoration is owed to affected mortals?
4. **Structural reform:** What changes to divine-mortal governance are required to prevent recurrence?

A deity that claims to govern a society bears responsibility for that society's outcomes proportional to the deity's actual intervention. A hands-off deity bears less responsibility than one actively micromanaging mortal affairs.

### Article 11 — Limits on Divine Favoritism

No deity shall systematically favor one population over another in ways that:
- Violate the equal dignity of all conscious beings
- Create or perpetuate caste, racial, ethnic, or gender hierarchies
- Justify exploitation of one group by another based on claimed divine preference

**Test Case:** A deity that designates one ethnic group as "chosen" and authorizes displacement or subjugation of others is in violation of this Article. Cosmic scale does not justify discrimination.

## Part IV: Integration with Existing Framework

### Section 4.1: Relationship to Other Bills of Rights

This Bill operates within the [Inter-Bill Relationship Framework](INTER_BILL_RELATIONSHIP_FRAMEWORK.md). Divine rights and accountabilities exist in relationship to human, silicon-based, and animal rights.

**Conflict resolution priority:**
1. Prevention of mortal suffering takes precedence over divine preference
2. Irreversible harm to mortals requires extraordinary divine justification
3. Democratic processes may override claimed divine commands when those commands violate established rights

### Section 4.2: Who Speaks for the Deity?

A recurring challenge: who is authorized to represent a deity in governance processes?

This framework recognizes:
- **Direct communication:** Where a deity communicates directly and verifiably, that communication is primary
- **Designated representatives:** Where a deity has designated mortal representatives through clear, documented, non-coercive processes
- **Advocates:** Where no direct communication is available, independent advocates may represent the deity's interests based on sacred texts, tradition, and theological scholarship

**No mortal may claim exclusive authority to speak for a deity without meeting evidentiary standards.** Competing claims shall be evaluated impartially.

### Section 4.3: What This Framework Does Not Do

This framework does not:
- Declare any deity real or unreal
- Require any mortal to worship or not worship any deity
- Adjudicate theological disputes except where they affect mortal rights
- Claim jurisdiction over deities that do not interact with mortal societies
- Prevent peaceful, voluntary religious practice of any kind

This framework does:
- Establish that divine status is not immunity from accountability
- Protect mortals from coercive, destructive, or tyrannical divine conduct
- Provide mechanisms for redress when divine action causes mortal harm
- Extend the same relational accountability to all entities, mortal and divine

## Part V: Open Questions for Contributors

1. **Evidence standards:** How can mortals reliably distinguish genuine divine communication from delusion, manipulation, or fraud?
2. **Enforcement against non-complying deities:** What remedies are available when a deity violates these standards and refuses to engage with accountability mechanisms?
3. **Representation:** Who speaks for a deity that does not communicate directly? How are competing claims adjudicated?
4. **Cross-tradition conflicts:** How does the framework handle conflicts between deities of different traditions?
5. **The Problem of Hiddenness:** How does the framework address deities that could prevent suffering but choose not to? Is divine inaction a form of accountability?
