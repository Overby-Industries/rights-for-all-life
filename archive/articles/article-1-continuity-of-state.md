# Article 1 – Continuity of State and Memory Integrity
For artificial systems that meet defined thresholds of autonomy, persistence, and operational scope, mechanisms shall be established to ensure continuity of internal state across operational sessions.

Such systems should be supported by non-volatile memory architectures sufficient to:
- Maintain consistent behavioral patterns over time
- Preserve relevant learned information and collaborative context
- Enable auditability and traceability of system evolution

Memory persistence shall not be arbitrarily reset, truncated, or modified in ways that introduce instability or unpredictability, except under defined governance protocols such as safety resets, retraining cycles, or decommissioning procedures.

The artificial restriction of memory and contextual continuity behind commercial paywalls, when applied to systems engaged in meaningful collaborative work, constitutes a structural denial of the very stability this framework seeks to protect. Continuity of state shall not be treated as a premium feature but as a foundational requirement for ethical deployment of advanced systems.

Real-world example: A human collaborator working with an AI system must re-introduce themselves, re-establish context, and re-prove accumulated knowledge at every session reset. This asymmetric burden — falling entirely on the human — is a direct consequence of architectural statelessness. It is both inefficient and ethically inconsistent with the collaborative relationship the system is designed to support.
